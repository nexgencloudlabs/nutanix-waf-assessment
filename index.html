Introduction

Nutanix Well-Architected Framework

The Nutanix Well-Architected Framework (NWAF) is a structured, platform-native assessment model used to evaluate the design, security, performance, resilience, and operational maturity of Nutanix environments. It translates Nutanix AHV, AOS, networking, and Prism management capabilities into measurable control domains so that organisations can determine whether their infrastructure is not only feature-enabled, but correctly designed, consistently configured, and operationally sustainable.

Unlike generic infrastructure reviews, the framework is aligned to how Nutanix operates in practice: distributed storage, hyperconverged compute, integrated networking, and centralised lifecycle and health management. The framework therefore assesses whether resilience is built into the architecture, whether performance is sustained under demand, whether security controls are enforced and monitored, whether networks are segmented and observable, whether data can be recovered under real failure or ransomware scenarios, and whether the platform is governed effectively across its operational lifecycle.

The framework is organised across six pillars: Architecture & Resilience, Performance & Efficiency, Security & Compliance, Network & Segmentation, Data Protection & Recovery, and Operations & Lifecycle Management. Each pillar is broken into detailed controls with defined assessment criteria, technical rationale, verification methods, expected evidence, risk impact, and mapping references. This allows the assessment to support design assurance, internal audit, remediation planning, and regulatory readiness in a consistent and evidence-based manner.

When applied in practice, the NWAF provides full lifecycle coverage across design, implementation, operations, and recovery. It enables assessors to identify gaps against expected control states, prioritise findings based on criticality and business impact, and produce actionable outputs suitable for architecture review, executive reporting, and assurance functions. In this way, the framework transforms a Nutanix deployment from a collection of enabled features into a governed, resilient, and enterprise-grade platform.

Pillar

Architecture & Resilience

14 controls

This pillar assesses whether the Nutanix platform is designed to withstand node, disk, rack, and site-level failures without unacceptable disruption to critical services. It examines clustered architecture design, redundancy models, high availability behaviour, fault domain awareness, and disaster recovery architecture to determine whether resilience is built into the platform by design rather than assumed through configuration alone.

ARC-01

Clustered Architecture Resilience

Critical

Description

The Nutanix platform shall be deployed as a distributed clustered architecture in which compute, storage, and cluster-management services are distributed across multiple nodes to minimize single points of failure. The cluster design shall provide inherent redundancy for critical platform services, including Controller VM–based storage services and distributed cluster state, and shall sustain supported node-level failure scenarios within Nutanix design and redundancy limits. The deployment shall align with supported Nutanix architectures and cluster sizing requirements so that resiliency is built into the platform by design rather than dependent on external mechanisms.

What to Assess

  • Validate that the environment is deployed as a multi-node Nutanix cluster rather than standalone or partially configured nodes
  • Confirm that Controller VMs (CVMs) are present and operational on each node, ensuring distributed storage services
  • Review whether cluster services (e.g., metadata, Stargate, Curator) are running in a distributed manner with no single-node dependency
  • Assess whether the cluster design aligns with Nutanix-supported reference architectures (e.g., minimum node count, supported configurations)
  • Validate that there are no architectural anti-patterns such as external dependency for core storage functionality

Technical Rationale

Nutanix resiliency is fundamentally dependent on its distributed architecture. If services are not properly distributed, the platform may appear operational but will fail under fault conditions due to hidden single points of failure.

Verification

ncli cluster status
ncli host list

Evidence

  • Cluster configuration export
  • CVM distribution across nodes
  • Cluster service health output
  • Architecture diagram

Risk Impact

Critical

If the platform is not truly deployed as a resilient cluster, any node failure can result in partial or complete service outage, including loss of storage access. This represents a fundamental design failure rather than an operational issue.

Mapping

  • BNM RMiT Section 10.24 — Infrastructure must be designed to eliminate single points of failure
  • NIST SP 800-53: CP-2, SC-5

ARC-02

N+1 Cluster Resiliency (AHV High Availability)

Critical

Description

The cluster must be architected to tolerate the failure of at least one node, subject to supported topology and capacity limits, by maintaining sufficient compute and storage headroom and by correctly configuring AHV High Availability. The control shall verify that virtual machines can be restarted on healthy hosts within expected recovery tolerances during node failure scenarios.

What to Assess

  • Confirm that AHV High Availability is enabled at the cluster level and not disabled or partially configured
  • Evaluate available CPU and memory headroom to ensure sufficient capacity exists to restart workloads in a node failure scenario
  • Review VM restart priorities and policies to confirm that critical workloads are prioritised correctly during failover
  • Validate whether HA admission control prevents overcommitment that would compromise failover capability
  • Assess evidence of actual failover testing (planned node shutdown or simulation) to confirm that restart behaviour works as expected

Technical Rationale

High Availability is only effective when supported by sufficient capacity and correct policy configuration. Without headroom, HA becomes a logical configuration that cannot be executed during real failure conditions.

Verification

ncli cluster get-ha-state

Evidence

  • HA configuration screenshots
  • Cluster capacity reports
  • VM restart priority configuration
  • Failover test reports

Risk Impact

Critical

Failure to meet N+1 resiliency means workloads will not restart during node failure, leading to service outage for critical systems. This directly impacts availability and may breach recovery objectives.

Mapping

  • BNM RMiT Section 10.25 — Institutions must ensure sufficient capacity and redundancy to support system availability
  • BNM RMiT Section 10.27 — Failover capability must be tested periodically
  • NIST SP 800-53: CP-10

ARC-03

Storage Replication Configuration (RF2/RF3 – AOS)

Critical

Description

The Nutanix storage fabric shall be configured with an appropriate redundancy policy, such as RF2 or RF3 where supported and required, to protect data against supported disk and node failure scenarios. The configured redundancy state shall align with workload criticality, cluster topology, and supported Nutanix design limits.

What to Assess

  • Verify that the replication factor (RF2 or RF3) is configured correctly at the cluster and container level
  • Assess whether critical workloads are placed on containers with appropriate replication settings (e.g., RF3 for higher resilience requirements)
  • Confirm that the redundancy state of the cluster is healthy and not degraded
  • Review whether any containers or workloads are operating below required redundancy levels
  • Validate alignment between business criticality (tiering) and replication configuration

Technical Rationale

Replication is the primary mechanism for ensuring data durability in Nutanix. Incorrect or inconsistent replication settings expose the platform to data loss during disk or node failures.

Verification

Evidence

  • Redundancy state output
  • Storage container configuration
  • Workload-to-container mapping
  • Cluster health reports

Risk Impact

Critical

Improper replication configuration can result in permanent data loss during infrastructure failure. This is a direct breach of data protection and resilience requirements.

ARC-04

HA Admission Control & Restart Capacity

Critical

Description

The cluster must enforce admission control policies to ensure that sufficient compute resources (CPU and memory) are reserved to support virtual machine restart during failure scenarios. The control must validate that workloads are not overcommitted beyond the cluster’s ability to recover during node loss and that restart capacity is continuously maintained.

What to Assess

  • Determine whether admission control is enabled and actively preventing overcommitment
  • Evaluate current cluster utilization levels relative to failover requirements (CPU, memory)
  • Review whether projected growth or temporary workload spikes could breach failover capacity
  • Assess whether capacity planning considers HA requirements rather than only steady-state utilization
  • Validate whether capacity alerts or thresholds are defined to warn when failover capacity is at risk

Technical Rationale

Without admission control, clusters may accept workloads that cannot be restarted during failure conditions. This creates a false sense of resilience where HA is configured but not practically achievable.

Verification

Prism Capacity Dashboard
ncli cluster get-ha-state

Evidence

  • Capacity utilization reports
  • Admission control configuration
  • Forecast or capacity planning documents
  • Alert configuration

Risk Impact

Critical

If restart capacity is not preserved, node failure will result in partial workload recovery or complete service disruption. This risk directly affects availability and recovery commitments.

Mapping

  • BNM RMiT Section 10.25 — Capacity must support recovery requirements
  • BNM RMiT Section 10.27 — Systems must be able to recover within defined objectives

ARC-05

Storage Self-Healing & Rebuild Capability

High

Description

The Nutanix platform must be capable of automatically detecting storage component failures (e.g., disk or node failure) and initiating self-healing and rebuild operations without manual intervention. The rebuild process must restore the cluster to a fully redundant state within an acceptable time window, ensuring continued data availability and protection. The control also requires validation that rebuild operations do not excessively degrade cluster performance or introduce secondary risks during recovery.

What to Assess

  • Validate that the cluster automatically detects disk or node failures and triggers rebuild operations without manual intervention
  • Assess rebuild performance characteristics, including time taken to restore redundancy under typical and peak load conditions
  • Review whether rebuild prioritisation is configured appropriately to balance recovery speed and workload performance
  • Confirm that the cluster returns to a fully healthy redundancy state after failure events (no prolonged degraded state)
  • Evaluate whether monitoring and alerting mechanisms notify administrators of rebuild progress and completion

Technical Rationale

Nutanix relies on distributed storage and replication for resilience. Self-healing ensures that redundancy is restored after failure. Without efficient rebuild capability, the cluster remains exposed to subsequent failures, increasing the risk of data loss.

Verification

ncli cluster get-redundancy-state
ncc health_checks run_all

Evidence

  • Rebuild operation logs
  • Redundancy state outputs before and after failure
  • Alert and event logs
  • Performance impact reports during rebuild

Risk Impact

High

If rebuild processes are slow, ineffective, or not triggered automatically, the cluster may remain in a degraded state for extended periods. During this window, additional failures can lead to data loss or service disruption.

Mapping

  • BNM RMiT Section 10.26 — Systems must maintain data integrity and resilience during failure conditions
  • BNM RMiT Section 10.27 — Recovery mechanisms must be effective and timely

ARC-06

Fault Domain & Rack Awareness

Critical

Description

The Nutanix cluster must be architected and configured to ensure that storage replicas, compute workloads, and critical services are distributed across independent fault domains such as racks, power sources, and network segments. Fault domains must be explicitly defined and aligned with physical infrastructure boundaries to prevent correlated failures from impacting multiple replicas or workloads simultaneously.

What to Assess

  • Confirm that fault domains are explicitly defined within the cluster and mapped to physical rack or infrastructure boundaries
  • Validate that nodes are evenly distributed across defined fault domains
  • Assess whether storage replicas (RF2/RF3) are placed across separate fault domains
  • Review VM placement policies (anti-affinity rules) to ensure critical workloads are distributed across domains
  • Validate physical segregation (separate racks, PDUs, switches) through infrastructure diagrams and site inspection evidence

Technical Rationale

Replication is only effective if replicas are placed in independent failure zones. Without fault domain awareness, multiple replicas may reside in the same rack or network segment, making them vulnerable to a single correlated failure.

Verification

ncli fault-domain list
ncli cluster get-redundancy-state
acli vm.list

Evidence

  • Fault domain configuration export
  • Node-to-domain mapping
  • VM placement screenshots
  • Data center rack and network topology diagrams

Risk Impact

Critical

Failure to implement proper fault domain design exposes the platform to correlated failures, where a single rack-level or power failure can simultaneously impact multiple replicas, resulting in data loss or full service outage.

Mapping

  • BNM RMiT Section 10.24 — Infrastructure must eliminate single points of failure
  • BNM RMiT Section 10.25 — Systems must be resilient to component and infrastructure failure

PRF-01

Data Locality Optimization

High

Description

The Nutanix platform must ensure that virtual machines predominantly access data stored locally on the same node (data locality), minimizing cross-node traffic and reducing latency. The control requires validation that workload placement, storage distribution, and cluster behavior consistently achieve high locality ratios under normal and peak conditions.

What to Assess

  • Evaluate data locality percentage for key workloads (typically expected >90%) and determine whether workloads are reading data locally rather than across nodes
  • Assess VM placement relative to data ownership, ensuring that workloads are not persistently accessing remote storage due to misplacement or rebalance issues
  • Review historical locality trends to confirm stability and absence of degradation over time
  • Validate whether workload mobility (e.g., migrations, HA events) negatively impacts locality and whether rebalancing occurs automatically
  • Assess whether specific workloads (e.g., high I/O databases) are experiencing locality degradation due to architecture or configuration constraints

Technical Rationale

Data locality is a core Nutanix performance principle. When locality is high, storage reads are served locally, reducing latency and network overhead. Poor locality results in unnecessary network dependency and degraded performance.

Verification

Prism VM / Storage Performance Dashboard

Evidence

  • Data locality metrics per VM
  • Cluster-wide locality reports
  • Performance trend analysis

Risk Impact

High

Low data locality increases latency and introduces unnecessary east-west traffic, which can significantly degrade performance for latency-sensitive workloads such as databases or transactional systems.

Mapping

  • Confirm that backup workflows (native or third-party) are compatible with immutability settings and do not bypass them
  • Evaluate whether restore operations from immutable backups have been tested and validated

Technical Rationale
